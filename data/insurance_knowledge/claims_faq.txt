INSURANCE CLAIMS — FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

Q: How do I file a motor insurance claim in Sri Lanka after an accident?
A:
1. Ensure everyone is safe; call ambulance/police if needed
2. Do NOT move vehicles until police arrive if injuries are involved
3. Take photos of damage, positions of vehicles, licence plates
4. Exchange details with other party (name, NIC, insurer, policy number)
5. Report to your insurer within 24–72 hours (check your policy)
6. File a police report at the nearest police station (B-report)
7. Submit claim form + driving licence + vehicle registration book + police report
8. Insurer's surveyor will inspect; take vehicle to approved garage

Q: Can my health insurance claim be rejected in Sri Lanka?
A: Yes. Common reasons:
— Pre-existing condition not disclosed at proposal
— Treatment during waiting period
— Excluded condition (cosmetic surgery, self-inflicted, etc.)
— Non-network hospital without pre-authorization
— Incomplete documentation
— Fraudulent claim

Q: What happens if I miss a life insurance premium payment?
A:
— Grace period: 30 days (annual/semi-annual), 15 days (monthly) — policy remains in force
— After grace period: Policy lapses if less than 3 premiums paid (no surrender value)
— After 3+ premiums paid: Policy becomes paid-up (reduced coverage continues)
— Revival: Within 2 years typically by paying all arrears + interest + health declaration

Q: What is surrender value and how is it calculated?
A:
Surrender value = amount paid if you cancel your life policy early.
— Available after paying premiums for minimum 2–3 years (varies by insurer)
— Guaranteed Surrender Value (GSV) = approximately 30% of total premiums paid (after year 3)
— Special Surrender Value is usually higher: based on paid-up SI × surrender value factor
— Bonus accumulations are also partially included

Q: What documents do I need for a life insurance death claim?
A:
— Completed claim form
— Original policy document
— Death certificate (from Registrar General's Dept, Sri Lanka)
— NIC of deceased and nominee
— Medical records / hospital discharge summary
— Post-mortem report (if accidental or suspicious death)
— FIR / police report (if accident-related)
— Nominee's birth certificate (if minor)
— Proof of relationship (marriage certificate if spouse; birth certificate if child)

Q: What is the difference between comprehensive and third-party motor insurance?
A:
Third-Party Only (Compulsory):
— Covers: Death/injury to others, damage to others' property
— Does NOT cover: Your own vehicle damage, your own medical expenses
— Cheapest option; legally required in Sri Lanka

Comprehensive:
— Covers everything in third-party PLUS your own vehicle damage
— Includes: fire, natural disasters, theft (if specified)
— More expensive but recommended for vehicles under 10 years old

Q: How does No-Claim Discount (NCD) work?
A:
— NCD rewards claim-free years with a discount on the OD (own damage) component
— Sri Lanka scale: 10% (year 1), 20%, 30%, 40%, 50% (max, year 5+)
— Making any claim = NCD resets to 0% at next renewal
— NCD certificate from current insurer needed to transfer discount to a new insurer
— NCD only applies to Own Damage premium, not Third-Party premium

Q: What is a critical illness rider?
A:
A rider (add-on) to a life or health policy that pays a lump sum if you are diagnosed with a specified serious illness. Common covered conditions:
— Cancer (usually malignant tumours, specified stages)
— Heart attack (first heart attack of specific severity)
— Stroke (permanent neurological deficit)
— Kidney failure (requiring dialysis)
— Major organ transplant
— Coronary artery bypass surgery
— Paralysis
Payment is usually a one-time lump sum; the policyholder can use it for treatment, income replacement, or any purpose.

Q: What is co-payment in health insurance?
A:
Co-payment (co-pay) means the policyholder shares the claim cost with the insurer. Example: 20% co-pay means you pay 20% of every claim, insurer pays 80%. Policies with higher co-pay have lower premiums.

Q: How do I check if my insurer is licensed in Sri Lanka?
A:
Visit the IRCSL website (www.ircsl.gov.lk) and check the "Licensed Insurers" list. Always verify before purchasing.

Q: What is premium financing?
A:
Some banks and finance companies offer loans to pay insurance premiums (especially for large commercial policies). The policy is assigned to the financier as security.

Q: What is the difference between a life insurance agent and broker?
A:
Agent: Represents ONE insurer. Works on commission from that insurer. Bound to sell that insurer's products only.
Broker: Independent professional representing the CLIENT. Surveys the market, compares products from multiple insurers, recommends the best fit. Must hold IRCSL broker licence. May charge a fee or receive commission from insurer.
