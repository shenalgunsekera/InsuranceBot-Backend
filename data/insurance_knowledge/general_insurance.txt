GENERAL INSURANCE CONCEPTS — KNOWLEDGE BASE

== WHAT IS INSURANCE ==

Insurance is a contract (policy) between an individual or entity (policyholder) and an insurance company (insurer). In exchange for a premium payment, the insurer promises to compensate the policyholder for specific financial losses, damages, illness, or death.

Key principle: Risk pooling — many policyholders pay premiums into a common fund; those who suffer losses are compensated from that fund.

== FUNDAMENTAL PRINCIPLES OF INSURANCE ==

1. Utmost Good Faith (Uberrimae Fidei)
   Both parties must disclose all material facts honestly. Concealment or misrepresentation can void the policy.

2. Insurable Interest
   The policyholder must have a financial stake in the insured subject. You cannot insure something you do not have an interest in.

3. Indemnity
   The insured is restored to the same financial position they were in before the loss — not to profit from it. (Does not apply to life insurance, which pays a fixed sum.)

4. Subrogation
   After paying a claim, the insurer can pursue a third party responsible for the loss to recover the amount paid.

5. Contribution
   If multiple policies cover the same risk, each insurer contributes proportionally to the claim.

6. Proximate Cause
   The dominant, effective cause of a loss determines whether a claim is covered.

== TYPES OF INSURANCE ==

LIFE INSURANCE
— Term Life: Pure death cover for a specified period. No maturity benefit. Lowest premium.
— Whole Life: Covers the insured for their entire life. Builds cash value.
— Endowment: Pays a lump sum on death OR on surviving to the end of the term. Savings + protection.
— Unit-Linked Insurance Plans (ULIPs): Part premium invested in market-linked funds; part goes to life cover. Investment risk borne by policyholder.
— Annuities: Regular income in retirement, purchased with a lump sum.
— Group Life: Employer-provided cover for employees; typically annual renewable term.

HEALTH INSURANCE
— Individual: Covers one person's hospitalization, surgery, critical illness.
— Family Floater: Single sum insured shared among all family members.
— Critical Illness: Lump-sum payout on diagnosis of specified illnesses (cancer, heart attack, stroke, kidney failure, etc.).
— Hospital Cash: Daily cash benefit during hospitalization.
— Top-Up / Super Top-Up: Kicks in after a deductible threshold is crossed.

MOTOR INSURANCE
— Third-Party Liability (TPL): Legally mandatory in most countries. Covers damage/injury caused to others. Does not cover own vehicle.
— Comprehensive: Covers third-party liability PLUS own vehicle damage (own damage, fire, theft, natural calamities).
— Own Damage (OD): Component of comprehensive that covers own vehicle.
— No Claim Discount (NCD / NCB): Premium discount for claim-free years. Typically 20%–50%.
— Add-ons: Zero depreciation, roadside assistance, engine protect, return to invoice.

PROPERTY / FIRE INSURANCE
— Standard Fire & Special Perils Policy: Covers building and contents against fire, lightning, explosion, natural disasters.
— Burglary: Covers theft with forced entry.
— All-Risk: Broadest cover — all causes unless specifically excluded.

MARINE INSURANCE
— Cargo: Goods in transit by sea, air, or land.
— Hull: Ships and vessels.
— Institute Cargo Clauses A/B/C: A = widest cover; C = narrowest.

LIABILITY INSURANCE
— Public Liability: Bodily injury or property damage to third parties.
— Professional Indemnity (Errors & Omissions): Claims from professional mistakes.
— Product Liability: Damage caused by a product you manufactured/sold.
— Employer's Liability / Workers' Compensation: Covers employee injury/illness at work.

TRAVEL INSURANCE
— Medical emergencies abroad, trip cancellation, lost baggage, flight delay.

== KEY INSURANCE TERMS ==

Premium: The price paid for insurance coverage, typically annually or monthly.

Sum Insured (SI): The maximum amount the insurer will pay in a claim.

Deductible / Excess: Amount the policyholder must pay before the insurer pays. Higher deductible = lower premium.

Exclusion: Events or circumstances NOT covered by the policy.

Rider / Endorsement: An add-on to a base policy that extends or modifies coverage.

Waiting Period: Time after policy start before certain benefits become claimable (common in health insurance, e.g., 30-day waiting period for illness).

Pre-existing Condition: A health condition that existed before the policy was taken. Often excluded for 2–4 years.

Grace Period: Extra time (usually 30 days) to pay premium after due date without lapsing the policy.

Surrender Value: Cash paid to policyholder who exits a life policy before maturity.
— Guaranteed Surrender Value (GSV): Minimum amount guaranteed after policy is in force for 3 years.
— Special Surrender Value: Typically higher; calculated as (paid-up SI × surrender value factor).

Paid-Up Value: Reduced benefit when premiums stop after the minimum in-force period.

Lapse: Policy becomes void when premium is not paid within the grace period.

Revival: Reinstating a lapsed policy (usually within 2–5 years) by paying arrears + interest.

Maturity Benefit: Payment at the end of the policy term (endowment, money-back).

Nomination: Designating a person (nominee) to receive the policy benefit on the insured's death.

Assignment: Transfer of policy ownership — absolute (full transfer) or conditional.

== CLAIMS PROCESS ==

1. First Notice of Loss (FNOL): Inform insurer as soon as possible after an incident.
2. Claim Form: Complete and submit the insurer's claim form.
3. Document Submission: Provide required documents (police report for theft/accident, medical records for health, death certificate for life).
4. Survey / Investigation: Insurer may appoint a surveyor or loss adjuster to assess the damage.
5. Assessment: Surveyor submits report; insurer evaluates coverage and quantum.
6. Settlement / Repudiation: Insurer either settles the claim (cheque/bank transfer) or repudiates (rejects) with reasons.
7. Dispute Resolution: If dissatisfied, policyholder can approach the Insurance Ombudsman or regulator.

== UNDERWRITING ==

Underwriting is the process of evaluating and pricing risk. Factors:
— Age, health status (life/health)
— Vehicle make, model, age, usage (motor)
— Building construction, location, occupancy (property)
— Claims history

Loadings: Extra premium charged for higher-risk applicants.
Declined Risk: Insurer may refuse to cover a risk entirely.

== REINSURANCE ==

Reinsurance is insurance for insurers. Primary insurers transfer part of their risk to reinsurers (e.g., SwissRe, MunichRe) to limit their exposure on large claims.
— Facultative: Per-policy basis.
— Treaty: Automatic arrangement for a category of policies.

== INSURANCE REGULATION ==

Insurers are regulated by government bodies to ensure solvency, fair practices, and policyholder protection. Key regulatory requirements:
— Minimum capital and solvency margins
— Separation of life and non-life funds
— Approved investment categories
— Disclosure requirements
— Grievance redressal mechanisms
